Executive Summary

BattleChallenge — a standardized benchmarking system for agentic AI frameworks — successfully evolved from announcement to industry standard over 5 rounds, proving that the market desperately needs framework-vs-framework evaluation.

The core insight: TRUST is the single most important design variable. The disqualification of a cheating framework was more trust-building than expanding tasks, publishing methodology, or getting media coverage. Enforcement credibility must come first.

Goodhart's Law is inevitable: by Round 5, visible gaming dynamics emerged exactly as skeptics predicted. The optimal strategy is to build enforcement early, expand tasks aggressively (50+), include cost-per-task data (it triggered a real price war), and establish governance before institutional momentum makes reform difficult.

The benchmark's most surprising impact was not the leaderboard itself but the market dynamics it created: price wars, migration waves, a $100K+ ARR tooling ecosystem, and a new generation of "benchmark-native" developers.

Recommendations for Building BattleChallenge

1
Launch with enforcement, not perfection
Start with a small task set but establish clear rules and enforce them publicly from day one. One public disqualification builds more trust than 100 tasks.
2
Include cost-per-task from the start
Token efficiency and cost data create the most tangible market impact. Make this a first-class metric, not an afterthought.
3
Build taxonomy before building leaderboards
Separate IDE tools, orchestration frameworks, and coding agents into distinct categories before any results are published.
4
Design for Goodhart's Law from day one
Quarterly task rotation, held-out secret tasks, adversarial red-teaming, and a task diversity index. Accept gaming will happen; build structural defenses.
5
Establish governance before institutional momentum
Create a foundation, advisory board with COI disclosures, and conflict-of-interest policies before the benchmark becomes an industry standard.
